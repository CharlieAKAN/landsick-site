Mario Casas Gets Back Behind the Camera

The News

Mario Casas is back in the director’s chair. His new film, A puño descubierto, has begun shooting in Barcelona. This is his second feature as a director after Mi soledad tiene alas.

Casas is not just directing. He is also starring in the film with his brother, Óscar Casas. The story follows two brothers caught in the world of underground fights. One wants out. One is still trapped inside. That is simple, tense, and very filmable.

The shoot started on April 17, 2026, and is set to run for six and a half weeks. Filming is taking place in Barcelona, around Catalonia, and in Gran Canaria. The film is produced by Nostromo Pictures and A puño descubierto AIE, with Movistar Plus+ involved. Warner Bros. Pictures Spain will distribute it in Spanish theaters in 2027.

Why We Care

This is the kind of project we like to watch closely. Not because it is loud. Because it is focused.

A brother story. A fight world. A director who is also acting. That setup gives us pressure from every side. If the film works, it will not be because of size. It will be because every choice points at the same wound.

That is a lesson for us as filmmakers. You do not always need a huge concept. You need a strong engine. Here, the engine is blood, loyalty, and the cost of trying to leave the life you were handed.

The Craft Angle

The crew list is worth noting. Edu Canet is the director of photography. Núria Guardia is handling production design. Javier Frutos is editing. Alba S. Torremocha is composing the music.

That tells us Casas is building the film around tone, not just performance. A movie about underground fighting can go cheap fast if it only chases punches. The real work is in the spaces between the hits: locker rooms, quiet streets, bad apartments, family silence, and the look someone gives before they make the wrong choice.

For us, that is where the movie lives. Not in the fist. In the pause before it lands.

What We Can Learn

If you are making your own film, steal the simple lesson: keep the story close to the body.

Two brothers is enough. One world is enough. One impossible choice is enough. When the frame is that tight, every camera move has to earn its place. Every cut has to carry weight. Every location has to tell us who these people are before they speak.

That is good filmmaking. Not fancy. Just honest.
