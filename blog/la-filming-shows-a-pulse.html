LA Filming Shows a Pulse

The Story

FilmLA dropped its Q1 2026 production report on April 28, and the headline is simple: Los Angeles filming is not fully back, but feature films are moving again.

On-location production in Greater LA hit 5,121 shoot days from January through March 2026. That is up 10.7% from the last quarter of 2025, but still down 3.3% from Q1 2025.

The bright spot is feature films. That category hit 687 shoot days in Q1 2026, up 45.2% from the prior quarter and up 52.3% from the same time last year. For anyone who has watched LA sets slow down, that number feels like a pulse.

Why It Matters

We care because shoot days are not just numbers. They are grips loading trucks. They are PAs getting a day rate. They are locations being rented, gear being booked, permits being pulled, and small vendors staying alive.

FilmLA also said California tax credit projects made up 21.8% of feature film shoot days and 17.1% of television shoot days in Greater LA during the quarter. That tells us incentives are not some abstract policy thing. They are shaping where productions actually roll cameras.

The Catch

This is not a victory lap. Television is still hurting. FilmLA reported TV production at 1,196 shoot days, down 28.4% from Q1 2025. Reality TV took the biggest hit, falling 52.2% year over year.

So yes, features are climbing. But the wider production ecosystem is still uneven. A healthy LA industry needs more than one category to wake up.

What Filmmakers Should Watch

The most useful part of this story is the local push to make filming easier. FilmLA pointed to LA’s new Low Impact Permit Pilot Program, built to lower permit costs and speed up the process for smaller shoots.

That matters for us. Not every production is a studio feature with a giant locations team. A lot of real filmmaking happens with lean crews, tight days, and one shot at getting the scene. If LA can make that process simpler, smaller productions may have a better shot at staying local.

Our Take

This week’s signal is clear: LA is not dead as a film town. But it has to fight for every shoot day now.

For filmmakers, the lesson is practical. Follow the incentives. Watch the permit rules. Build plans that can move fast. The productions that survive right now are the ones that know the creative side and the logistics side.

Because getting the shot is still the goal. But getting the shot permitted, staffed, insured, and budgeted is the game.
