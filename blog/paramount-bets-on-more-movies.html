Paramount Bets on More Movies

The News

Paramount told shareholders on May 4, 2026 that it has nearly doubled its film slate, moving from 8 films in 2025 to 15 films in 2026. The studio also said it is still committed to releasing at least 30 films a year across Paramount and Warner Bros. if its Warner Bros. Discovery deal closes.

That sounds like a big win for movie theaters. More films means more release dates, more trailers, more crews, more vendors, and more chances for audiences to show up.

But there is a catch. Paramount also said it expects significantly lower theatrical revenue in 2026, even with more releases. The reason: lower average box office revenue per film as the studio builds toward its 2027 and later slates.

Why Filmmakers Should Care

This is the part that matters for us on the ground. A bigger slate does not always mean bigger swings. It can mean more mid-sized bets, more franchise upkeep, more concert films, more horror, more comedy, and more titles built to feed theaters first, then streaming and licensing later.

For working filmmakers, that shift can cut both ways. More films can mean more jobs and more open doors. But lower average box office per film can also mean tighter budgets, faster schedules, and more pressure to prove the audience before the camera even rolls.

The Theatrical Window Is Back in the Conversation

Paramount said every film in the combined Paramount and Warner Bros. plan would get a full theatrical release with a minimum 45-day window. That is a real signal. After years of studios testing shorter windows and streaming-first plays, this puts the theater back at the center of the plan.

For us, that changes how we think about the work. A theatrical-first film has to hold up big. The sound mix matters. The opening image matters. The pacing matters. You are not just making something to be half-watched on a couch while someone checks their phone.

The Bigger Lesson

The industry is not moving in one clean line. Studios want more movies in theaters, but they also want safer bets. They want scale, but they want control. They want a bigger slate, but they are warning investors not to expect every title to break out.

So the lesson for filmmakers is simple: make work that knows its audience. If you are pitching a feature, a branded doc, or even a short proof of concept, be clear about who it is for and why they will care now. Taste still matters. Craft still matters. But the business side is asking harder questions earlier.

More movies is good news. More pressure is part of the deal. Our job is to make the kind of work that can survive both.
